How does coral bleaching happen?

Image from www.shutterstock.com

The Mechanism

Corals live with symbiotic algae that provide most of their energy. When sea temperatures rise or conditions become stressful, corals expel these algae. Without the algae’s pigments, corals appear white (“bleached”).

Bleaching severity

The percentage shows how much of a coral colony has bleached:

  • <1% — virtually no bleaching; reef condition is normal.
  • 1–10% — low-level stress; most colonies recover when temperatures drop.
  • 10–50% — moderate bleaching; recovery depends on how long heat stress lasts.
  • 80%+ — severe; colonies are unlikely to recover and many die.

Coral Reef Regions

Each region can have its own local factors driving coral bleaching.

  • Indo-Pacific: Largest area; frequent severe bleaching events.
  • Caribbean: Episodic events often worsened by disease.
  • Indian Ocean: Wide-scale events like 2016 heatwave.
  • Red Sea: Some resilience but still vulnerable to extremes.

What we see

Depth and turbidity show only weak relationships with coral bleaching intensity. However, exposure — how much wave energy and light a site receives — can affect stress levels to some extent. Reefs in shallow, high-light areas tend to bleach more quickly, while deeper or more sheltered sites sometimes fare better.

Final Thoughts

Coral bleaching reveals how fragile ocean ecosystems have become under rising global temperatures. While mild bleaching can reverse, frequent and severe events leave little time for recovery. Protecting coral reefs means tackling climate change, reducing local pressures, and restoring damaged habitats.

Coral reefs sustain biodiversity, fisheries, and coastal protection for millions. Preserving them is vital for both marine life and human communities.
